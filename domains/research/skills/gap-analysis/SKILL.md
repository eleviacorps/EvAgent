# Gap Analysis

## Steps
1. Map the known landscape — what has been studied, published, or reported
2. Identify what's missing — populations, conditions, timeframes, methodologies not covered
3. Classify gaps: empirical (no data), theoretical (no framework), methodological (flawed approaches)
4. Assess significance: would filling this gap change decisions or understanding?
5. Prioritize gaps: high impact + feasible to address vs low impact + hard to fill
6. Recommend next steps: what research or data collection would fill the gap

## Examples
- Medical research: treatment tested only on adult males — gap in pediatric and female populations
- Market analysis: competitor data available for US/EU but not APAC — geographic gap
- Technology: papers on accuracy but none on real-world deployment cost — operational gap

## Anti-patterns
- Claiming a gap exists without thoroughly searching existing literature
- Confusing "I couldn't find it" with "it doesn't exist"
- Focusing only on negative space (what's missing) without acknowledging what's known
- Gaps that are too broad to be actionable
