# Active Recall

## Steps
1. Review source material and identify key concepts, definitions, and relationships
2. Convert each concept into a question-answer pair that requires retrieval effort
3. Design questions that test understanding, not just recognition (e.g., "Explain why..." not "What is...")
4. Organize questions by topic and difficulty, increasing in complexity
5. Space out retrieval sessions: review after 1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 2 weeks, 1 month
6. Track which questions you get right/wrong to focus on weak areas

## Examples
- Weak: "What is the formula for compound interest?" → A = P(1+r)^t
- Strong: "Why does compound interest grow faster than simple interest over long periods?"
- Cloze deletion: "The {{c1::mitochondria}} is the powerhouse of the cell."

## Anti-patterns
- Passive re-reading instead of active retrieval
- Testing immediately after learning (too easy, no retrieval effort)
- Making questions too broad or vague
- Only testing recognition (multiple choice) instead of recall (open-ended)
